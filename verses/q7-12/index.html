Imami Tafseer Imam ʿAlī (ع) — Nahj al-Balāgha, Khuṭba 192 (al-Qāsiʿa) · Al-Kāfī · Tafsīr al-Mīzān (Ṭabāṭabāʾī)

Imam ʿAlī's Khuṭba al-Qāsiʿa (Nahj al-Balāgha, Sermon 192) provides the foundational Imami reading of Q 7:12 — and it is extraordinary in its theological precision. Imam ʿAlī identifies the Iblīsic refusal as the archetype of a recurring structural pattern throughout history: the substitution of self-derived comparative evaluation (anā khayrun minhu) for obedience to divine command. Iblīs did not deny God's existence, God's authority, or the fact of God's command. Iblīs accepted all three — and then substituted his own rational assessment ("fire is superior to clay") for compliance. The refusal operates entirely within a framework of acknowledged divine authority, which is precisely what makes it structurally distinctive.

فَعَدُوُّ اللَّهِ إِمَامُ الْمُتَعَصِّبِينَ وَسَلَفُ الْمُسْتَكْبِرِينَ الَّذِي وَضَعَ أَسَاسَ الْعَصَبِيَّةِ وَنَازَعَ اللَّهَ رِدَاءَ الْجَبَرُوتِ

Nahj al-Balāgha, Khuṭba 192 — Imam ʿAlī (ع): "The enemy of God is the Imam of the fanatical and the forefather of the arrogant — he who laid the foundation of tribal partisanship (ʿaṣabiyya) and contended with God over the mantle of supreme power."

Imam ʿAlī identifies three dimensions of the Iblīsic act in the Qāsiʿa sermon: (1) al-ʿaṣabiyya — tribal/category partisanship, the privileging of one's own ontological category (fire over clay) as the basis for refusing divine command; (2) al-istikbār — arrogance, the self-elevation that places one's own assessment above divine designation; (3) contention with God over divine prerogatives — the claim to evaluate divine appointments by one's own standard. These three are the structural anatomy of every subsequent refusal of the divinely-designated ḥujja.

Ṭabāṭabāʾī in al-Mīzān makes the theological point precise: Iblīs's error was not a factual claim. It may be true that fire is "hotter" than clay in some physical sense. The error was the inference: that fire's comparative physical quality constitutes a superior ontological station that overrides divine designation. This is the fundamental category error — substituting material ontology for divine designation as the criterion of authority. Adam's khalīfa-status came from divine appointment (Q 2:30), not from material composition. Iblīs's refusal was a refusal to recognize that divine designation constitutes authority, not material superiority.

Akbarian School Ibn ʿArabī — Al-Futūḥāt al-Makkiyya · Fuṣūṣ al-Ḥikam (Fass Ādam) · Sermon 192 cross-reference

Ibn ʿArabī's reading of Iblīs in Al-Futūḥāt and the Fass Ādam of Fuṣūṣ al-Ḥikam focuses on the metaphysical failure of Iblīs as a mazhar (locus of divine manifestation). Iblīs possessed knowledge — he had worshipped God for ages, had access to divine proximity, and understood the structure of creation. His failure was not ignorance but a defect at the level of kashf (direct spiritual perception): he perceived Adam's material composition (clay) and could not perceive the divine reality that clay was chosen to contain. Iblīs's perception was ẓāhir-only — he saw the outer form of Adam's materiality and missed the inner bāṭin: Adam as the vessel of the complete divine names (Q 2:31).

Ibn ʿArabī: Iblīs is the paradigm of the being who has ʿilm (knowledge) without kashf (direct perception). He knows the theological categories but cannot see the reality they point to. This is the precise opposite of the walī al-kāmil (perfect friend of God), who has kashf — direct perception of divine reality — as the condition of perfect knowledge. The Iblīsic defect is: formal theological knowledge + absence of kashf = the most dangerous spiritual condition, because it produces confident wrong judgment grounded in real but incomplete information.

The statement anā khayrun minhu is, for Ibn ʿArabī, the declaration of a failed mazhar: Iblīs claimed to be a superior vessel of divine manifestation, when in fact Adam was designated by God as the most complete vessel of divine names in creation. The refusal to prostrate was a refusal to recognize God's own self-disclosure in the form God chose. Iblīs thus opposed God's tajallī (self-disclosure) in the name of his own superior theological assessment. This is the defining structure of anti-walāya: opposing the divinely-designated mazhar on the grounds of one's own evaluation of who is more deserving.

Synthesis — Mullā Ṣadrā and the Nafs-Inversion Mullā Ṣadrā — al-Asfār al-Arbaʿa · Tafsīr al-Qurʾān al-Karīm · Ḥaydar Āmulī synthesis

Mullā Ṣadrā's reading of Q 7:12 operates through his doctrine of al-ḥaraka al-jawhariyya (substantial motion): every being moves constantly toward either greater ontological intensity (wujūd-intensification) or toward ontological diminishment. The Iblīsic act — anā khayrun minhu — represents the decisive moment of directional inversion: the being who was moving toward greater wujūd-proximity to God chose at the moment of trial to prioritize self-comparative evaluation over divine command. This inversion locks the trajectory: the nafs (soul/self) that chooses anā over compliance does not simply make a single error — it sets its trajectory in the direction of increasing self-enclosure, away from the wujūd-source.

The Ṣadrian analysis makes the Iblīsic structure understandable as a process: (1) knowledge of divine command — present; (2) capacity for compliance — present; (3) nafs-orientation (hawā al-nafs) toward self-comparative evaluation — present and decisive; (4) the nafs-orientation overrides the will-toward-compliance. This is the precise mechanism of what Q 45:23 calls: أَفَرَأَيْتَ مَنِ اتَّخَذَ إِلَٰهَهُ هَوَاهُ — "Have you seen one who has taken his desire as his deity?" Iblīs made anā (self-assessment) his deity — the criterion by which even divine commands are evaluated.

The Convergence

Imami tradition (Khuṭba al-Qāsiʿa): anā khayrun minhu = the founding act of ʿaṣabiyya and istikbār — the archetype of every refusal of the divinely-designated ḥujja. Akbarian tradition: Iblīs is the paradigm of ʿilm without kashf — formal knowledge without direct perception of divine reality, producing confident anti-walāya. Ṣadrian synthesis: the Iblīsic act is a nafs-inversion that sets the trajectory away from wujūd-source — a one-way ontological door. The synthesis point: the Iblīsic pattern is not an external evil but an internal nafs-orientation that can occur in any being who has theological knowledge (a dangerous proximity, not a safe distance). Those most at risk of the Iblīsic pattern are not the ignorant but the theologically knowledgeable who evaluate divine designations by their own criteria — the Nawāṣib pattern (Q 22:46: hearts that do not perceive through the eye of the qalb).
