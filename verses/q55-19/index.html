Imami Tafseer Tafsir al-Burhan (al-Bahrani) Vol. 5 · Tafsir al-Qummi (Ali ibn Ibrahim) · Bihar al-Anwar Vol. 24 · Tafsir Nur al-Thaqalayn (al-Huwayzi) · Tabatabai, al-Mizan Vol. 19

Surah al-Rahman (Q 55) is distinguished by its repeated refrain — fa-bi-ayyi ala rabbikuma tukadhdhibani (so which of the favors of your Lord would you deny?) — addressed throughout in the dual form (kuma), speaking to two addressees simultaneously. Classical tafseer debates who the two are: humanity and jinn, or some other pair. The Imami tradition reads the dual address as the structural key to the surah: it speaks throughout to a dual reality, and Q 55:19-22's two seas is the surah's own central image of that duality.

Tafsir al-Burhan (al-Bahrani, Vol. 5) records the identification from Imam al-Sadiq (peace be upon him):

الْبَحْرَانِ عَلِيٌّ وَفَاطِمَةُ ۖ وَالْبَرْزَخُ رَسُولُ اللَّهِ صَلَّى اللَّهُ عَلَيْهِ وَآلِهِ ۖ وَاللُّؤْلُؤُ وَالْمَرْجَانُ الْحَسَنُ وَالْحُسَيْنُ

Tafsir al-Burhan Vol. 5 — Imam al-Sadiq (peace be upon him) on Q 55:19-22

"The two seas are Ali and Fatima; the barrier (barzakh) is the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him and his family); and the pearl and coral are al-Hasan and al-Husayn." This identification transforms the entire passage into a compressed theological statement of the Ahl al-Bayt's ontological structure.

Maraja al-bahrayn yaltaqiyan — "He released the two seas, meeting side by side": God released them — they did not seek each other by their own initiative. The divine act of releasing (maraja) is the same as the divine designation (nass): God brought together Ali and Fatima (peace be upon them) by His own act and will. They meet (yaltaqiyan) — their union is a meeting of two complete, distinct realities, not the merger of two partial things into one. Ali (peace be upon him) is a complete sea; Fatima (peace be upon her) is a complete sea. Their meeting does not dissolve either into the other.

Baynahuma barzakhun la yabghiyan — "between them is a barrier so that neither transgresses." The barzakh is the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him and his family). The Prophet (peace be upon him) is the divine principle that stands between the two seas, maintaining each in its own nature, preventing either from overwhelming the other. Neither sea transgresses against the other — because the barzakh ensures the integrity of both. This is the Quranic image of the divine wisdom that paired Ali and Fatima: the Prophet's station is the mediating principle that makes their union possible without dissolving their distinct natures.

Tafsir al-Qummi (Ali ibn Ibrahim) records a parallel narration connecting the surah's dual addressee to this verse: the two seas are the two foundations of the post-prophetic order — Ali as the foundation of zahir authority (Imamic governance, jurisprudence, the zahir of walaya) and Fatima as the foundation of batin nur (the transmission of prophetic light into the Imamic chain, the nur that passes through her). The barzakh-Prophet ensures they remain distinct functions while remaining united in one household.

Yakhruju minhuma al-lu'lu wa-al-marjan — "from both of them emerge the pearl and the coral." From two complete seas, two things emerge — lu'lu (pearl) and marjan (coral). Bihar al-Anwar (Vol. 24) and Tafsir Nur al-Thaqalayn record the identification: al-lu'lu is al-Hasan (peace be upon him) and al-marjan is al-Husayn (peace be upon him). The distinction between the two is not one of value — both pearl and coral are precious — but of nature and mode. The pearl (lu'lu) is formed inside the oyster in the depths of the sea — hidden, formed in darkness over time, perfectly white and translucent. Al-Hasan (peace be upon him) represents this mode: his walaya was interior, his Imama characterised by strategic withdrawal and preservation of the community, his light the hidden white light that illuminates from within. The coral (marjan) is formed in the sea but visible on the surface, red in colour, its structure exposed and permanent. Al-Husayn (peace be upon him) represents this mode: his walaya was manifested externally at Karbala, red with the colour of martyrdom, permanently visible in history as the standing structure of sacrifice that future generations see and recognise.

Tabatabai in al-Mizan (Vol. 19) develops the theological significance of la yabghiyan — "neither transgresses." In Quranic usage, baghiy is the transgression of one party against another's rights — the violation of proper limits. The two seas of Ali and Fatima (peace be upon them) have distinct, God-given natures and functions. The divine barzakh (the Prophet) ensures these distinct natures are preserved. Neither transgresses against the other because both are held in their proper ontological position by the divine intermediary. Tabatabai: "This is the Quran's image of a divinely ordered household — not the merger of two persons into one but the meeting of two distinct realities, each complete in itself, held in proper relationship by the divine will."

Akbarian School Ibn Arabi, al-Futuhat al-Makkiyya Vol. 1 (barzakh chapter) · Fusus al-Hikam (Fass of Adam — barzakh as constitutive ontological principle) · Abd al-Razzaq al-Qashani, Tawilat al-Quran on Q 55:19

The barzakh (isthmus/barrier) is one of Ibn Arabi's most developed technical concepts in al-Futuhat. He defines the barzakh as a reality that participates in both of the things it separates/connects without being reducible to either. It is neither the first thing nor the second thing — it is the third reality that makes the relation between the two possible. The barzakh does not simply prevent the two seas from merging — it is what constitutes the two seas as distinct and as related simultaneously. Without the barzakh, there would be no two seas: they would dissolve into undifferentiated water, or simply remain apart without relation.

Applied to Q 55:19-22 in the Akbarian framework: the two seas are two modes of divine self-disclosure (tajalli). Ibn Arabi identifies the two fundamental modes as jalal (divine majesty — overwhelming, severe, activating) and jamal (divine beauty — attractive, gentle, receiving). These two modes meet in creation but must be held in proper relation by the barzakh principle — without which jalal would overwhelm jamal or jamal would dissolve jalal. The pearl and coral that emerge — lu'lu wa marjan — are the fruits of the meeting of the two divine modes: jalal producing the coral (red, structured, outward) and jamal producing the pearl (white, hidden, interior).

Al-Qashani in Tawilat al-Quran makes the specific application: the divine feminine (al-untha al-ilahiyya) is the supreme mazhar of jamal — just as Ibn Arabi argued in the Fusus. The sea of jamal is the sea that produces the pearl — the hidden, interior, luminous fruit formed in the depths. The sea of jalal is the sea that produces the coral — the manifest, structured, permanent fruit visible on the surface. Both seas are necessary; both produce precious things; and the barzakh between them is what makes the emergence of both possible without the destruction of either mode.

Synthesis — Haydar Amuli and Mulla Sadra Sayyid Haydar Amuli, Jami al-Asrar wa Manba al-Anwar · Mulla Sadra, al-Asfar al-Arbaa Vol. 2 · Mulla Sadra, Mafatih al-Ghayb

Haydar Amuli's synthesis in Jami al-Asrar applies both the Imami identification and the Akbarian barzakh framework to produce a single coherent reading of Q 55:19-22. Ali (peace be upon him) is the sea of zahir walaya — the manifest, active, governing dimension of walaya: jurisprudence, Imamic authority, the zahir of the divine command exercised in creation. Fatima (peace be upon her) is the sea of batin nur — the hidden, transmitted, generative dimension of the prophetic light: she receives the prophetic light from above and transmits it as the constitutive nur of the Imamic chain below. These are two complete seas — not partial, not dependent on each other for completion, but distinct modes of a single divine reality.

The Prophet (peace be upon him and his family) as the barzakh: he is the mediating principle that constitutes both Ali and Fatima as what they are. Ali received from the Prophet the zahir of walaya — the Imamic authority formally designated at Ghadīr. Fatima received from the Prophet the batin of prophetic nur — the light that passes through her into al-Hasan and al-Husayn. The Prophet as barzakh is the reality that makes both transmissions possible without confusion: the zahir authority to Ali, the batin nur through Fatima. Neither transgresses against the other (la yabghiyan) because the Prophet's station holds each in its proper ontological function.

Convergence — Lu'lu and Marjan: Two Modes of Imamic Light from One Fatimid Source

The Imami identification (al-Hasan = lu'lu / al-Husayn = marjan) and the Akbarian framework (lu'lu = jamal mode / marjan = jalal mode) converge: al-Hasan (peace be upon him) and al-Husayn (peace be upon him) are two modes of the one Imamic light that emerges from the two seas of Ali and Fatima. The pearl is hidden, white, interior — formed in the depth of the sea: al-Hasan's Imama was the preservation of the community through strategic withdrawal, his walaya the interior white light that sustained the Shia community through the most dangerous period after Karbala's prerequisite. The coral is red, structured, externally visible, permanently present on the surface of the sea: al-Husayn's Imama was the external manifestation at Karbala — the red of martyrdom that stands permanently in history as the coral stands permanently in the sea. Both emerge from both seas — from Ali (the zahir sea) and from Fatima (the batin sea) — because both received their constitution from both parents. Mulla Sadra: "The lu'lu and marjan are not two different things — they are two modes of one Imamic reality, one interior (hasan/preserved) and one exterior (husayni/manifested), both emerging from the same Fatimid source."

Mulla Sadra in Mafatih al-Ghayb adds the ontological precision: yakhruju minhuma — "from both of them" — not from one sea or the other but from both simultaneously. The Imams al-Hasan and al-Husayn (peace be upon them) receive their ontological constitution from both Ali and Fatima, and through Fatima from the prophetic light of the Prophet (peace be upon him). The phrase minhuma (from both of them) is the Quran's own confirmation that the Imamic constitution requires both the zahir sea (Ali) and the batin sea (Fatima) — neither alone is sufficient. This is the Quranic grounding of the theological principle that the Imam must be from the lineage of both Ali and Fatima, not merely Alid: the nur must emerge from both seas together.
