Imami Tafseer Al-Kafi (Imam al-Baqir, Imam al-Sadiq) · Tafsir al-Burhan · Tafsir al-Mizan (Tabatabai) · Bihar al-Anwar

Tabatabai in al-Mizan opens with a foundational methodological precision: the verse does not say "Allah is like a light" — it says "Allah is the light of the heavens and the earth." The mathal (likeness) that follows is not a comparison of God to a lamp. The mathal is a likeness of how God's light is disclosed in creation — not what God is in His essence. The mishkat, the misbah, and the zujaja are images of the mode of divine self-disclosure (tajalli). This distinction is ontologically critical: the verse describes the receptacle and transmission of divine light, not the light itself.

The Imami hadith literature on this verse is specific and convergent. Imam al-Baqir in Tafsir al-Burhan: "The mishkat (niche) is the heart of the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him and his family); the lamp (misbah) is the light of knowledge that Allah placed in his heart; the glass (zujaja) is Imam Ali ibn Abi Talib, who received that light from the Prophet and transmits it — he is the carrier of the light after the Prophet; the blessed olive tree neither eastern nor western is the lineage of Ibrahim, belonging to no single nation but universal."

الْمِشْكَاةُ قَلْبُ مُحَمَّدٍ صَلَّى اللهُ عَلَيْهِ وَآلِهِ وَالْمِصْبَاحُ النُّورُ الَّذِي أَوْدَعَهُ اللهُ فِي قَلْبِهِ وَالزُّجَاجَةُ عَلِيٌّ عَلَيْهِ السَّلَامُ

Tafsir al-Burhan — Imam al-Baqir (peace be upon him)

A second report from Imam al-Sadiq in Bihar al-Anwar adds the chain dimension: "Nurun 'ala nur — light upon light — means Prophet upon Imam, Imam upon Imam, each transmitting the divine light to the next, from Adam to the Imam of our age." The phrase nurun 'ala nur is the Quran's own description of the walaya-chain structure: each bearer of divine light illuminates the next, with no gap. The verse thus contains implicitly the complete doctrine of succession — each Imam as a glass (zujaja) that receives the prophetic light and transmits it forward without diminishment.

Tabatabai draws the theological corollary from "Allah guides to His light whomever He wills" (yahdi Allahu li-nurihi man yasha'): guidance to the divine light is not automatic or unmediated. It requires the appointed wasila — the mishkat in which the lamp burns. To reject the mishkat is to reject the only vessel through which the divine light reaches creation. The verse contains, implicitly, the entire walaya doctrine: the Imam is the ontological condition of spiritual illumination, not an optional spiritual aid.

Akbarian School Ibn Arabi, al-Futuhat al-Makkiyya · Fusus al-Hikam · Abd al-Razzaq al-Qashani, Ta'wilat al-Quran

Ibn Arabi's reading of Q 24:35 begins from the ontological claim that nur (light) is not a metaphor in this verse — it is a precise ontological description. In al-Futuhat al-Makkiyya, Ibn Arabi argues: existence (wujud) and light (nur) are convertible terms. That which exists is luminous to the degree that it exists; non-existence is darkness. "Allah is the Light of the heavens and the earth" means: Allah is Existence Itself — al-Wujud al-Mutlaq — and all existence in creation is a participation in and a reflection of that primordial light. The verse is therefore the Quran's most compressed ontological statement: divine existence is the light through which all created existence is possible.

The mishkat, on the Akbarian reading, is the Insan al-Kamil — the Perfect Man. Ibn Arabi in al-Futuhat: the Perfect Man is the supreme locus (mazhar) through which the divine light reaches creation in its most complete form. Without the mazhar of the Insan al-Kamil, the divine light — though ontologically present throughout creation — cannot be received by created beings in its wholeness. The glass (zujaja) is the Pure Heart (qalb safi) of the Perfect Man, polished to the degree that it transmits rather than refracts the light. The brilliant star (kawkabun durriyyun) is the individuated manifestation of the divine light in the particular Insan al-Kamil of each age.

The phrase "neither eastern nor western" (la sharqiyyatin wa-la gharbiyyatin) receives decisive Akbarian attention. Al-Qashani in Ta'wilat al-Quran: the blessed tree whose oil almost lights itself is positioned beyond all relational categories — beyond the duality of east/west, zahir/batin, past/future. The Insan al-Kamil transcends all positional duality because he is the point of convergence between the divine and the created — the barzakh (isthmus) that belongs to neither side but connects both. His knowledge is 'ilm ladunni — divine knowledge from God's presence, not derived from any human tradition positioned in the east or west of history.

Nurun 'ala nur in the Akbarian reading is the convergence of two ontological modes: Nur al-Wujud (the light of existence — the ontological light through which anything exists at all) and Nur al-'Irfan (the light of recognition — the epistemic light through which existence recognizes itself as divine). The Insan al-Kamil is the being in whom these two lights converge: he exists most fully (Nur al-Wujud at its highest creaturely intensity) and recognizes this existence as divine self-disclosure (Nur al-'Irfan as kashf). This double light is "light upon light" — existence recognizing itself in the mirror of the Perfect Man.

Synthesis — Haydar Amuli and Mulla Sadra Sayyid Haydar Amuli, Jami' al-Asrar wa Manba' al-Anwar · Mulla Sadra, Tafsir al-Quran al-Karim (Mafatih al-Ghayb)

Sayyid Haydar Amuli's contribution to Q 24:35 is the formal identification that completes the synthesis: the Akbarian Insan al-Kamil (the mishkat, the supreme mazhar of divine light) is the ma'sum Imam of Imami theology. The identification is not an allegory or approximation — it is an ontological equivalence. Both traditions, starting from different vocabularies, describe the same theological reality: there is a being in each age who is the supreme locus of divine self-disclosure, whose existence is constitutive of creation's order, and through whom alone the divine light can be received in its wholeness. The Imami tradition calls this being the Imam; the Akbarian tradition calls this being the Insan al-Kamil. Haydar Amuli proves they are one description, not two beings.

The Convergence at Q 24:35

Imami hadith (Imam al-Baqir): the mishkat = the Prophet's heart; the misbah = prophetic knowledge; the zujaja = Imam Ali as transmitter. Akbarian (Ibn Arabi / Qashani): the mishkat = the Insan al-Kamil as supreme mazhar; the lamp = Nur al-'Irfan; the glass = the purified qalb. Haydar Amuli's synthesis: these are the same identification at two levels of vocabulary. The "glass" (Imam Ali in Imami hadith; the Pure Heart of the Insan al-Kamil in Akbarian) is one being — the Imam as Insan al-Kamil. The convergence proves that walaya and wilaya al-wujudiyya (ontological closeness to God) are the same reality described in two traditions.

Mulla Sadra's contribution in Tafsir al-Quran (Mafatih al-Ghayb) is the proof that divine light and wujud are identical. Sadra's al-Hikma al-Muta'aliya establishes: wujud is light; the intensity of wujud in a being is the degree of its luminosity. Non-existence is darkness not as a spatial property but as the absence of wujud. Allah is the Light of the heavens and the earth means: Allah is Wujud al-Mutlaq — Absolute Existence — and the heavens and earth exist as participations in that Light. The Imam, as the being whose wujud is most intense among created beings — because he is the supreme mazhar of the divine names — is the created being who is most luminous, closest to the primordial light, and most capable of transmitting it to others.

Sadra's further point from al-Asfar: the prophetic light does not diminish as it transmits. The misbah in the zujaja does not become dimmer because it illuminates the glass. The Nur that the Prophet placed in Imam Ali's heart — and that the Imam transmits to each successive Imam — does not diminish through transmission. This is what nurun 'ala nur means structurally: each transmission of the prophetic light to the next Imam is an accumulation, not a dilution. The Imam of the age holds the prophetic light plus all the lights of the Imams between the Prophet and himself — the chain is cumulative, not linear-diminishing.

The practical implication Amuli and Sadra both draw: the guidance phrase — "Allah guides to His light whomever He wills" — means that divine guidance (hidaya) to the light is mediated through the mishkat. One cannot reach the divine light by bypassing the mishkat. The rejection of the Imam-as-mishkat is therefore not merely a theological error — it is the ontological condition of remaining in darkness regarding the divine light. Q 24:35 is the Quran's own statement of the walaya-condition of spiritual illumination.
