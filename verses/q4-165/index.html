Imami Tafseer Tafsīr al-Mīzān (Ṭabāṭabāʾī) · Al-Kāfī (Kitāb al-Ḥujja) · Shaykh al-Mufīd · Al-Ṭūsī

Ṭabāṭabāʾī in al-Mīzān identifies the theological structure of Q 4:165 as a divine statement of purpose (taʿlīl): God sends messengers for the reason that (liʾallā) human beings would otherwise have a legitimate argument (ḥujja) against God. The verse thus states explicitly that God's provision of prophetic guidance is motivated by God's refusal to allow the human claim: "We were not guided, therefore we cannot be held accountable." This is the divine preemption of the human counter-argument — the divine ḥujja must precede human accountability.

The term ḥujja carries full legal-theological weight: a proof, an argument, a case that cannot be rebutted. Q 6:149 confirms: فَلِلَّهِ الْحُجَّةُ الْبَالِغَةُ — "To God belongs the conclusive proof." God's case against creation is conclusive (bāligha) precisely because God has first provided everything creation needed to comply. The provision of prophets — and, the Imami tradition adds, the provision of Imams — is what makes God's case conclusive and the human counter-argument impossible.

لَا تَبْقَى الْأَرْضُ بِغَيْرِ إِمَامٍ لِئَلَّا تَحِجَّ الْأَرْضُ عَلَى اللَّهِ

Al-Kāfī, Kitāb al-Ḥujja — Imam al-Ṣādiq (ع): "The earth does not remain without an Imam, lest the earth itself have an argument against God."

This Al-Kāfī tradition extends the Q 4:165 argument across time: the purpose of the prophetic sequence was to prevent the human ḥujja — and the same logic requires that the Imam continue after the last prophet to prevent the same argument arising in every subsequent age. If the earth could exist without a living ḥujja, creation would have a valid complaint against God: "After the Prophet died, You left us without guidance." Al-Kāfī's tradition attributes to Imam al-Ṣādiq: إِنَّ الْأَرْضَ لَا تَخْلُو مِنْ حُجَّةٍ لِلَّهِ عَلَى النَّاسِ — "The earth is never devoid of a ḥujja of God upon the people."

Shaykh al-Mufīd and al-Ṭūsī crystallize this into the luṭf-argument: Q 4:165 states the principle (provide guidance lest creation have an argument), and the Imam is the application of that principle to the post-prophetic era. The absence of an Imam would mean the argument of Q 4:165 applies in reverse: creation would have a legitimate ḥujja against God for the post-prophetic period. This is impossible given divine wisdom. Therefore the Imam must exist in every age.

Akbarian School Ibn ʿArabī — Al-Futūḥāt al-Makkiyya · Fuṣūṣ al-Ḥikam (Fass Ibrāhīm)

Ibn ʿArabī approaches Q 4:165 through the concept of the divine self-disclosure (tajallī) that constitutes the prophetic mission. God does not leave creation without a mazhar (locus of divine manifestation) — because the divine names require created vessels through which to become manifest. The prophet is the supreme mazhar of the divine names of guidance, mercy, and wisdom in a given age. The verse's statement that God sends prophets "lest people have an argument" is, in Akbarian reading, simultaneously a statement about human need and about divine self-disclosure-necessity: God's own nature as al-Hādī (the Guide), al-Raḥmān (the Merciful), and al-Ḥakīm (the Wise) requires that these names become manifest through the prophetic mazhar.

In the Fuṣūṣ al-Ḥikam, the Fass of Ibrāhīm addresses the relationship between divine knowledge and human guidance: God knows what humanity needs before humanity knows it, and the prophetic mission is God's response to humanity's need before it is even consciously articulated. This is the ḥujja made conclusive before it is tested — the divine pre-provision of what humanity requires. On this reading, Q 4:165's "lest people have an argument" is not a reactive divine move (responding to a human complaint) but a proactive divine completeness: God fulfills the condition of guidance before accountability is applied.

Ibn ʿArabī further identifies the khatm al-awliyāʾ (seal of the saints/friends) as the continuing locus of the ḥujja-function after the seal of prophethood. The prophetic mazhar culminates in the last prophet; the walāya-mazhar continues in the awliyāʾ chain. Both serve the Q 4:165 function: ensuring that creation never lacks the living manifestation of divine guidance that defeats the human counter-argument.

Synthesis — Ḥaydar Āmulī and Mullā Ṣadrā Ḥaydar Āmulī — Jāmiʿ al-Asrār · Mullā Ṣadrā — Mafātīḥ al-Ghayb · al-Asfār al-Arbaʿa

Mullā Ṣadrā reads Q 4:165 through the lens of ontological necessity: divine proof (ḥujja) is not merely an evidentiary category — it is a structural necessity of existence. For Ṣadrā, divine guidance is the actualization of human rational capacity from potentiality to full actuality. Human rational beings are created with the potential for complete actualization of their divine-origin capacities; the prophetic mission is the external cause that catalyzes this actualization. Without the prophetic ḥujja, human rational capacity remains in potentiality — unable to actualize itself fully without the corresponding actualized model. Q 4:165 thus states an ontological necessity: creation requires the prophetic ḥujja in order to actualize its own divinely-given potential.

The Convergence

Imami tradition: the earth never lacks a ḥujja (Al-Kāfī) — the Q 4:165 argument extends across all time to require a living Imam in every age. Akbarian tradition: the divine names of guidance require a mazhar in every age — the khatm al-awliyāʾ continues the prophetic mazhar-function. Ḥaydar Āmulī synthesis: Ibn ʿArabī's khatm al-awliyāʾ = the Imam of each age in Imami theology. The function is identical: the living locus through whom the divine ḥujja is completed in each age, ensuring that neither the human counter-argument nor the divine names' requirement for self-disclosure goes unmet. Q 4:165 is therefore the Quranic ground of both the Imami doctrine of the permanent ḥujja and the Akbarian doctrine of the permanent mazhar — one verse, two vocabularies, the same ontological necessity.
