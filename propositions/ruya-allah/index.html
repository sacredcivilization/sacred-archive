Vocabulary register: Ruʾyat Allāh (vision of God — the disputed category); idrāk (encompassing perception — Q 6:103 uses this, not mere raʾā; the Imami distinction between idrāk-al-iḥāṭa and muṭlaq al-ruʾya is decisive); mushāhada (witnessing — the Sufi-Imami term for direct spiritual apprehension, higher than discursive knowledge); kashf (unveiling — the epistemological category for experiential certainty beyond rational inference); ʿayn al-yaqīn (the certainty of direct sight — one level above ʿilm al-yaqīn/knowledge-certainty and one below ḥaqq al-yaqīn/truth-certainty; the Quranic and Imami epistemological ladder); tajallī (divine self-disclosure / manifestation — Ibn ʿArabī's term for what actually occurs in any "vision of God": not the Essence [dhāt aḥadiyya] but a self-disclosure of a divine name); bilā kayf (without modality — the Ashʿarī formula for hereafter vision: affirmed but without specifying how); tawassul (seeking proximity through an intermediary — the kalām term for the theological function of the walī as a means of drawing near to God). Note on the core distinction: Q 6:103 uses lā tudrikuhu (does not encompass) — not lā tarāhu (does not see). Imami kalām exploits this: idrāk (encompassment) is impossible; the nature of the qalbī mushāhada is a separate, non-competing question.
RUYA-001 Grade A — Q 6:103 + Q 7:143 + Nahj al-Balāgha Sermon 1 Imami Layer I

Physical Vision of God Is Impossible — Q 6:103, Q 7:143, and Imam ʿAlī's Explicit Denial

Premise 1: Q 6:103: "Lā tudrikuhu al-abṣāru wa-huwa yudriku al-abṣāra wa-huwa al-Laṭīfu al-Khabīr" — "Eyes (abṣār) do not encompass (tudrik) Him, while He encompasses all eyes — He is the Subtle, the All-Aware." The verb is adrak/idrāk — encompassing perception, not mere raʾā (to see). The Imami distinction: idrāk = full comprehension with the faculty encompassing the object; this is what is denied. The divine attribute al-Laṭīf (the Subtle) confirms that God is not graspable by any finite faculty.

Premise 2: Q 7:143: Moses (ع) — one of the five Ulu al-ʿAzm prophets — explicitly requests: "Rabbi arinī anẓur ilayk" — "My Lord, show Yourself that I may look at You." God's response: "Lan tarānī" — "You shall never see Me." The particle lan in Arabic indicates categorical, permanent negation. Argument: if the greatest prophet before the Prophet Muḥammad ﷺ was categorically denied physical vision, the denial applies universally. The subsequent shattering of the mountain (jaʿalahu dakkan) confirms that created reality cannot withstand divine self-disclosure at the level of the Essence.

Premise 3: Imam ʿAlī (ع), Nahj al-Balāgha (Sermon 1): "Kadhdhabu man zaʿama annahu yarā Allāha bi-ʿayni raʾsihi" — "A liar is he who claims that he sees God with the eye of his head." This is one of the most direct Imami theological positions on record — not an inference but an explicit categorical statement from the first Imam. Al-Kāfī (Kitāb al-Tawḥīd, Imam al-Riḍā): "Al-wahmu lā yadrikuhu wa-al-khayālu lā yuṣawwiruhu" — "Imagination does not encompass Him; fantasy does not picture Him." All finite cognitive faculties — including physical sight, imagination, and fantasy — are excluded.

Conclusion: The Imami position on physical vision of God is categorical and multiply grounded: Q 6:103 (idrāk by eyes denied), Q 7:143 (lan tarānī — categorical negation even to Moses), Imam ʿAlī's explicit statement (Nahj al-Balāgha Sermon 1), and Imam al-Riḍā's comprehensive exclusion of all finite faculties (Al-Kāfī). Physical vision of God — in this world or the next — is impossible. This is a locked Imami theological position, not a matter of ijtihādī dispute.
Sources: Q 6:103; Q 7:143; Nahj al-Balāgha Sermon 1; Al-Kāfī, Kitāb al-Tawḥīd (Imam al-Riḍā, multiple chains); Shaykh al-Mufīd, Awāʾil al-Maqālāt (explicit denial of ruʾya); Ṭabāṭabāʾī, Al-Mīzān Vol. 7 (Q 7:143 commentary).
RUYA-002 Grade B — Muʿtazilī school (Qāḍī ʿAbd al-Jabbār, al-Mughnī) Muʿtazilī · Cross-School Layer I

The Muʿtazilī Argument — Vision Requires Direction and Corporeality: Both Are Impossible of God

Premise 1: Muʿtazilī rational argument (Qāḍī ʿAbd al-Jabbār, al-Mughnī): vision (ruʾya) requires that the seen object (marʾī) (1) exist in a specific direction (jiha) relative to the viewer, (2) be in a location (makān), and (3) be illuminated (mustanīr) or reflect light. God is not in any direction, has no location, and is not a physical body — therefore all conditions for vision are absent. Vision of God is not merely contingently absent but logically impossible.

Premise 2: Q 75:22-23 interpretation: "Wujūhun yawmaʾidhin nāḍiratun ilā rabbihā nāẓiratun" — Muʿtazilī taʾwīl: nāẓira here derives from al-naẓar bi-maʿnā al-intizār (waiting/expecting), not visual looking. Parallel: Q 16:33 uses naẓara for "waiting." Therefore: "faces on that day joyful, waiting for their Lord's [mercy]." No visual content is implied.

Premise 3: The Muʿtazilī and Imami positions converge on the denial of physical vision — but for different reasons. Muʿtazilī: rational impossibility based on God's non-corporeality. Imami: both Q 6:103/Q 7:143 nass AND rational impossibility, with the additional positive doctrine that qalbī mushāhada exists. The Muʿtazilī position eliminates both physical vision AND any experiential alternative; the Imami position eliminates only physical vision and preserves qalbī mushāhada as the positive category.

Conclusion: The Muʿtazilī denial of ruʾyat Allāh is grounded in the rational analysis of what vision requires (direction, location, corporeality) — none of which can apply to God. Their Q 75:22-23 taʾwīl (nāẓira = waiting) is exegetically coherent but not the only reading. Imami kalām agrees with the conclusion (physical vision impossible) while disagreeing on scope: Muʿtazilah eliminate all experiential access; Imami theology preserves qalbī mushāhada as the supreme mode of divine proximity. Cross-reference: RUYA-003 (Imami positive doctrine).
Sources: Qāḍī ʿAbd al-Jabbār, al-Mughnī fī Abwāb al-Tawḥīd wa-al-ʿAdl (ruʾya chapters); al-Zamakhsharī, al-Kashshāf (Q 75:22-23 Muʿtazilī tafsīr); Peters, God's Created Speech (Muʿtazilī theology overview); Frank, Beings and Their Attributes.
RUYA-003 Grade A — Nahj al-Balāgha + Al-Kāfī + Q 22:46 Imami Layer I · VI

The Imami Positive Doctrine — Qalbī Mushāhada: The Heart Sees What the Eye Cannot

Premise 1: Imam ʿAlī (ع), Nahj al-Balāgha / Biḥār al-Anwār: "Mā raʾaytu shayʾan illā wa-raʾaytu Allāha qablahu wa-baʿdahu wa-maʿahu wa-fīhi" — "I have not seen a thing without seeing God before it, after it, with it, and within it." This is not a statement of belief but of experienced certainty (ʿayn al-yaqīn). The Imam does not say "I believe God is present in all things" — he says "I see." The mode of seeing is qalbī, not baṣarī.

Premise 2: Q 22:46: "Fa-innahā lā taʿmā al-abṣāru wa-lākin taʿmā al-qulūbu allatī fī al-ṣudūr" — "Eyes do not go blind — but hearts within chests go blind." The Quran's hierarchy: the heart (qalb) is the primary organ of perception; the eye (baṣar) is secondary. Blindness of the heart is the real blindness; physical blindness (of the eye) does not affect the capacity for true perception. Corollary: if the heart is the primary organ of perception and the Quran warns specifically against its blindness, the heart is the faculty through which God — the highest object of perception — is apprehended.

Premise 3: Mullā Ṣadrā (al-Ḥikma al-Mutaʿāliya): the heart of the insān kāmil (perfected human) becomes the ʿarsh al-Raḥmān (the Throne of the Merciful). This is not metaphor — it is an ontological claim about what the purified heart becomes in the hierarchy of wujūd. The Imam's qalbī mushāhada is not a subjective experience but an ontological state: the Imam's heart has achieved the degree of wujūd at which divine tajallī is received directly. Cross-reference: MAQAM-001 (maqām of the Imam); IBN-AR-001 (tajallī doctrine).

Conclusion: The Imami theology of ruʾyat Allāh has two components: (1) denial — physical vision (baṣarī ruʾya) is categorically impossible (RUYA-001); (2) affirmation — qalbī mushāhada is the supreme mode of divine proximity, exemplified by Imam ʿAlī's experiential testimony in Nahj al-Balāgha and grounded in Q 22:46 (heart as primary organ of perception) and Mullā Ṣadrā's ontological analysis of the Imam's qalb. This is not a compromise between Muʿtazilī denial and Ashʿarī affirmation — it is a structurally different and more precise doctrine.
Sources: Nahj al-Balāgha (multiple sermons — 1, 186); Biḥār al-Anwār (Imam ʿAlī's testimonies); Al-Kāfī (Kitāb al-Tawḥīd); Q 22:46; Mullā Ṣadrā, al-Ḥikma al-Mutaʿāliya (ʿarsh al-Raḥmān); Muṭahharī, ʿIrfān-i Islāmī (qalbī mushāhada chapter).
RUYA-004 Grade B — Ashʿarī school (Bayḍāwī, Ghazālī) + Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī 574 Ashʿarī · Cross-School Layer I

The Ashʿarī Affirmation — Hereafter Vision Bilā Kayf: Grounded in Q 75:22-23 and the Full-Moon Ḥadīth

Premise 1: Ashʿarī position (al-Ashʿarī, al-Ibāna; al-Ghazālī, Iḥyāʾ ʿUlūm al-Dīn; al-Bayḍāwī, Anwār al-Tanzīl): Q 75:22-23 (ilā rabbihā nāẓiratun) is to be taken at face value — "looking at their Lord." The preposition ilā (toward) followed by nāẓira unambiguously indicates directional visual looking. The Muʿtazilī taʾwīl (nāẓira = waiting) is grammatically strained and motivated by theology, not linguistics: naẓara in classical Arabic followed by ilā means visual looking; naẓara meaning "waiting" takes no preposition or takes fī.

Premise 2: Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī 574 (multiple chains): The Prophet ﷺ said: "Innakum satarawna rabbakum ka-mā tarawna al-qamara laylata al-badr" — "You will see your Lord as you see the full moon — you do not compete with each other to see it." The Ashʿarī reading: this is a ḥadīth mutawātir (or near-mutawātir) that settles the question through naql. The full-moon simile (tashabbuhu al-ruʾya lā al-marʾī) — the comparison is to the clarity and ease of vision, not to the object seen: seeing God will be as clear and unobstructed as seeing the full moon.

Premise 3: Ashʿarī formula: bilā kayf (without modality). God will be seen but the manner is unknown and unknowable. This preserves: (1) the divine attribute of non-direction (lā jiha) — God is not in a place; (2) the affirmation of the ḥadīth and Q 75:22-23; (3) the limit of human cognition — we do not say "how." Imami critique of this formula: bilā kayf is simultaneously the formula's strength (humility about divine nature) and its weakness (epistemically empty — affirming something while refusing to specify any content gives no information about what is affirmed).

Conclusion: The Ashʿarī affirmation of hereafter vision bilā kayf is the dominant Sunni theological position, grounded in the apparent meaning of Q 75:22-23 and the full-moon ḥadīth (Bukhārī 574). Its internal tension: it simultaneously affirms that God has no direction (lā jiha) and that He will be seen directionally — the bilā kayf formula papers over this tension rather than resolving it. The Imami and Muʿtazilī critiques both target this tension: the Muʿtazilī by denying the vision entirely; the Imami by reframing the question (qalbī mushāhada, not baṣarī ruʾya). Cross-reference: RUYA-001 (Imami categorical denial of physical vision).
Sources: Al-Ashʿarī, al-Ibāna ʿan Uṣūl al-Diyāna; Al-Ghazālī, Iḥyāʾ ʿUlūm al-Dīn (Kitāb al-Tawba — vision of God section); Al-Bayḍāwī, Anwār al-Tanzīl (Q 75:22-23); Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī 574; Gimaret, La doctrine d'al-Ashʿarī.
RUYA-005 Grade B — Ibn ʿArabī, al-Futūḥāt al-Makkiyya + Q 2:115 Akbarian · Cross-School Layer I · VI

Ibn ʿArabī's Tajallī Framework — Vision Is of the Self-Disclosure, Not the Essence: Kullun Yarā Allāha fī Mirʾātihi

Premise 1: Ibn ʿArabī (al-Futūḥāt al-Makkiyya): the divine Essence (dhāt aḥadiyya) never discloses itself directly — even God does not "see" His own Essence, as the Essence has no mutaqābil (counterpart to face it). What appears in any "vision of God" is always a tajallī — a self-disclosure of a divine name or attribute into the cognitive field of the viewer. No created being has ever seen or will ever see the Essence qua Essence.

Premise 2: Q 2:115: "Fa-aynamā tuwallū fa-thamma wajhu Allāh" — "Wherever you turn, there is the face of God." The Akbarian reading: wajh Allāh (the face of God) = the self-disclosure (tajallī) of God present in every direction, in every existent. The verse grounds the universality of tajallī: one cannot look anywhere without encountering a divine self-disclosure. But this is the disclosure of names/attributes, not the Essence.

Premise 3: Ibn ʿArabī on Q 75:22-23 and hereafter vision: in the hereafter, every person will see God in the form (ṣūra) that their own theological belief (iʿtiqād) has shaped. The vision is real — but it is a tajallī shaped by the receptor's epistemic state. The person of tawḥīd sees a tajallī of the name al-Aḥad; the person of iʿtiqād of a particular divine form sees that form's tajallī. This explains why Q 75:22-23 affirms vision without contradiction: vision of divine tajallī is real; vision of the Essence remains impossible. Imami convergence: Ḥaydar Āmulī read this as consistent with Imam ʿAlī's qalbī mushāhada — different vocabulary, same ontological structure.

Conclusion: Ibn ʿArabī's tajallī framework dissolves the apparent contradiction between the Muʿtazilī denial and the Ashʿarī affirmation: both sides are arguing about "vision of the Essence" — which is indeed impossible (Muʿtazilī correct) — but Q 75:22-23 and the full-moon ḥadīth refer to vision of divine tajallī, which is real (Ashʿarī correct in affirmation, wrong in framing). The Akbarian synthesis converges with Imami qalbī mushāhada: the heart's purified mirror receives the tajallī; this is the highest mode of divine proximity available to created beings. Cross-reference: RUYA-003; IBN-AR-001–008.
Sources: Ibn ʿArabī, al-Futūḥāt al-Makkiyya (Bāb on ruʾya); Ibn ʿArabī, Fuṣūṣ al-Ḥikam (Faṣṣ Mūsā — the chapter on Moses directly addresses Q 7:143); Q 2:115; Q 75:22-23; Ḥaydar Āmulī, Jāmiʿ al-Asrār; Corbin, Alone with the Alone (Ibn ʿArabī on theophany).
RUYA-006 Grade B — Al-Kāfī (Kitāb al-Tawḥīd) + Ṭabāṭabāʾī, Al-Mīzān Imami · Cross-School Layer I · VI

The Imami Epistemological Ladder — ʿIlm al-Yaqīn, ʿAyn al-Yaqīn, Ḥaqq al-Yaqīn: Vision as the Second Degree

Premise 1: The Quranic epistemological ladder: Q 102:5-7: "Kallā law taʿlamūna ʿilma al-yaqīn — tharawnna al-jaḥīm — thumma latarawunnaha ʿayna al-yaqīn" — "If only you knew with certain knowledge — you would see Hellfire — then you would see it with the eye of certainty." Three degrees: (1) ʿilm al-yaqīn — certainty from evidence/inference; (2) ʿayn al-yaqīn — certainty from direct sight; (3) Q 56:95 adds ḥaqq al-yaqīn — certainty by becoming/being the thing itself. This ladder is the Imami epistemological framework for understanding any claim of divine proximity.

Premise 2: Imam ʿAlī's "mā raʾaytu shayʾan" testimony (RUYA-003) operates at the level of ʿayn al-yaqīn — not mere ʿilm al-yaqīn (belief/inference). This is why it is expressed as a statement of seeing (raʾaytu) rather than knowing (ʿalamtu). Al-Kāfī (Imam al-Ṣādiq): "Al-qalb ḥaramuhu wa-al-ʿaql amīruhu" — "The heart is His sanctuary and the intellect is its ruler." The heart is the faculty of ʿayn al-yaqīn; the intellect is the faculty of ʿilm al-yaqīn. Ḥaqq al-yaqīn belongs to the maʿṣūm state exclusively.

Premise 3: Ṭabāṭabāʾī (Al-Mīzān, commentary on Q 7:143): Moses's request was for ʿayn al-yaqīn — direct experiential sight. God's lan tarānī means: the mode of ʿayn al-yaqīn that Moses imagined (as physical sight) is impossible. But the mountain's shattering and Moses falling unconscious (wa-kharrar Mūsā ṣāʿiqan) represents what happens when ʿayn al-yaqīn arrives in a different mode — not as physical sight but as overwhelming direct presence. Moses fainted not because nothing happened but because something too great for his current state arrived. When he recovered: "Subḥānaka tubtu ilayk" — he achieved a degree of proximity precisely through the refusal.

Conclusion: The Imami epistemological ladder (ʿilm al-yaqīn / ʿayn al-yaqīn / ḥaqq al-yaqīn — Q 102:5-7, Q 56:95) provides the structural framework for understanding ruʾyat Allāh: (1) physical-baṣarī vision is denied (RUYA-001); (2) ʿayn al-yaqīn through qalbī mushāhada is the actual positive doctrine (RUYA-003) — exemplified by Imam ʿAlī and, in a preliminary form, by Moses's experience at the mountain; (3) ḥaqq al-yaqīn belongs to the maʿṣūm Imam's full state. The debate between Muʿtazilī denial and Ashʿarī affirmation is resolved: both sides were arguing about physical vision; the Imami doctrine transcends the binary by identifying the correct faculty (qalb, not baṣar) and the correct epistemological category (ʿayn al-yaqīn, not idrāk al-abṣār).
Sources: Q 102:5-7; Q 56:95; Q 7:143-144; Al-Kāfī (Kitāb al-Tawḥīd — multiple chapters on divine perception); Ṭabāṭabāʾī, Al-Mīzān Vol. 8 (Q 7:143 commentary); Muṭahharī, ʿIrfān-i Islāmī (epistemological ladder); Shaykh al-Mufīd, Awāʾil al-Maqālāt (explicit Imami denial of baṣarī ruʾya).
