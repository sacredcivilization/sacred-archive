Vocabulary register: Primary terms: hādī (the guide — root h-d-y: to guide, to show the way; the hādī is the one who walks ahead and shows the path by their own walking), mundhir (warner — the prophetic function: alerting to danger; completed with the Seal of Prophethood), imām (leader — one who stands in front; Q 17:71 — every community called bi-imāmihim — by their Imam), ḥujja (proof/argument — the Imam as divine proof on earth in each age; the one whose presence makes accountability possible), hidāya (guidance — two types: hidāya al-bayān = declarative guidance via message; hidāya al-tawfīq = ontological guidance via the Imam's walāya), ghayba (occultation — the Imam's hidden presence in the current age), al-Hādī (divine name — Allah as the ultimate Guide; the Imam as locus of this name's manifestation in the human world).
HADI-001 Grade A — Q 13:7 · Q 32:24 · Al-Kāfī (Imam al-Bāqir) Imami Layer I

The Prophet as Warner, the Imam as Guide — Two Distinct Divinely-Mandated Functions

Premise 1: Q 13:7: innamā anta mundhirun wa-li-kulli qawmin hādin — "You are only a warner (mundhir), and for every people a guide (hādin)." Two distinct functions named in the same verse: warning (the prophetic function — alerting humanity to divine norms and the consequences of their violation) and guiding (an ongoing function requiring a living locus in each age). The Prophet warns; the hādī guides.

Premise 2: Al-Kāfī (Imam al-Bāqir on Q 13:7): "al-mundhiru rasūlu Allāh wa-al-hādī ʿAlī wa-fī kulli zamānin imāmun minnā yahdī ilā mā jāʾa bih rasūlu Allāh" — "the warner is the Messenger of Allah, the guide is ʿAlī, and in every age there is an Imam from us who guides toward what the Messenger of Allah came with." The hādī function is structural — it does not end with the first hādī but continues through the Imamic chain in each age.

Premise 3: Q 32:24: "wa-jaʿalnā minhum aʾimmatan yahdūna bi-amrinā lammā ṣabarū wa-kānū bi-āyātinā yūqinūn" — "We made from among them leaders guiding bi-amri Allāh (by Our command)." The hādī guides not by scholarly authority or communal appointment but by divine command. Two conditions: patience (ṣabr) and certainty (yaqīn) in divine signs — qualities of the Imam, not elected or scholarly leadership.

Conclusion: Q 13:7 establishes the structural distinction between the prophetic warning function (completed with the Seal of Prophethood) and the guidance function (continuing through the Imams bi-amri Allāh). The hādī is not a political successor or scholarly authority but a divinely-mandated function maintaining the walāya-axis's operativity in each age. Without a living hādī, the guidance-function lapses — making recognition of the Imam of each age structurally necessary for access to living divine guidance.
Sources: Q 13:7; Q 32:24; Al-Kāfī, Kitāb al-Ḥujja (Imam al-Bāqir on Q 13:7).
HADI-002 Grade A — Q 21:73 · Q 2:124 · Al-Kāfī, Kitāb al-Ḥujja Imami Layer I

Divine Guidance is Channeled Through the Imam — Waḥy, ʿAhd, and the Exclusion of the Ẓālimīn

Premise 1: Q 21:73: "We made them leaders (aʾimma) guiding by Our command and inspired them (awḥaynā ilayhim) to do good deeds, establish prayer, and give zakāt." The Imams receive divine inspiration (waḥy in a non-prophetic form — called ilhām or taḥdīth in the Imami tradition). This distinguishes the Imam from the scholarly authority: guidance bi-amri Allāh requires divine connection, not merely scholarly excellence.

Premise 2: Q 2:124: Allah makes Abraham imāman li-l-nās (leader for the people) after Abraham passes the divine tests. Abraham asks: "And from my descendants?" Allah replies: "lā yanālu ʿahdī al-ẓālimīn" — "My covenant (ʿahd) does not encompass the ẓālimīn." The verse performs three functions simultaneously: separates prophethood and imāma as distinct functions; establishes imāma as divine covenant (ʿahd), not human appointment; explicitly excludes the ẓālimīn from legitimate imāma.

Premise 3: Al-Kāfī (Imam al-Ṣādiq on Q 2:124): the ẓālim who held the caliphate after the Prophet cannot be the legitimate hādī — Q 2:124 itself excludes anyone who has committed ẓulm from the Abrahamic imāma covenant. This connects the hādī question directly to the Saqīfa event: the criterion for legitimate hādī is divine appointment (ʿahd Allāh), not political consensus.

Conclusion: Q 21:73 establishes that divine guidance to the Imams includes a form of divine inspiration (awḥaynā ilayhim), distinguishing the Imam's function from scholarly authority. Q 2:124 establishes that imāma is a divine covenant (ʿahd) that explicitly excludes those who have committed ẓulm. Together: the hādī is neither elected nor appointed by scholarly consensus — they hold a divine covenant, receive divine inspiration, and are constitutionally excluded from being ẓālim.
Sources: Q 21:73; Q 2:124; Al-Kāfī, Kitāb al-Ḥujja (Imam al-Ṣādiq on Q 2:124); Ṭabāṭabāʾī, al-Mīzān, Vol. 1 (Q 2:124 full analysis).
HADI-003 Grade B — Al-Kāfī, Kitāb al-Ḥujja · Q 5:35 Imami Layer V

The Imam is the Embodiment of Guidance Itself — Not a Transmitter but a Living Hujja

Premise 1: Al-Kāfī (Imam al-Ṣādiq): "al-imāmu dalīlu Allāhi ʿalā khalqih" — "the Imam is Allah's proof upon His creation." The Imam is not a channel through which guidance passes (as a book transmits text) — the Imam is the living instantiation of divine guidance in the human world. Q 5:35: "O you who believe — have taqwā of Allah and seek wasīla to Him and strive in His path."

Premise 2: Al-Kāfī (Imam al-Bāqir on ʿilm al-Imam): the Imam possesses ʿilm al-manāyā wa-l-balāyā wa-faṣl al-khiṭāb — knowledge of deaths, trials, and the decisive word. This is not scholarly knowledge derived from study but the knowledge that flows from the Imam's ontological position as ḥujja Allāh on earth — the one whose presence maintains the intelligibility of divine guidance in the human world.

Premise 3: Al-Kāfī (Imam al-Ṣādiq): "al-arḍu lā takhlu min ḥujja" — "the earth is never without a ḥujja." The Imam's function is cosmic: without the living ḥujja, the created world loses its orientation axis. This is not mere political governance or scholarly leadership — it is an ontological function: maintaining the presence of divine walāya in the world of material creation.

Conclusion: The Imam is not a transmitter of guidance but the living embodiment of it — the ḥujja Allāh on earth whose presence maintains the walāya-axis in each age. Al-Kāfī's repeated formulations (dalīl Allāh, ḥujja Allāh, the earth never empty of ḥujja) establish this as a cosmic function, not merely a political or scholarly one. The distinction between the Prophet's text-transmission (Quran) and the Imam's living embodiment (walāya) is the distinction between hidāya al-bayān (declarative guidance) and hidāya al-tawfīq (ontological guidance).
Sources: Al-Kāfī, Kitāb al-Ḥujja (multiple hadith on Imam as ḥujja and dalīl); Q 5:35; Ṭabāṭabāʾī, al-Mīzān, Vol. 6 (Q 5:35 — wasīla as the Imam).
HADI-004 Grade A — Q 17:71 · Al-Kāfī · Biḥār al-Anwār Imami Layer VI

Every Community Called by Its Imam on Judgment Day — The Eschatological Centrality of the Hādī

Premise 1: Q 17:71: yawma nadʿū kulla unāsin bi-imāmihim — "The Day We call every people by their Imam." Not by their prophet, not by their scripture, not by their community name — by their Imam. The hādī's function is the organizing principle of eschatological identity. The Imam one recognized in this life determines the Imam by whose name one is called on the Day of Judgment.

Premise 2: Al-Kāfī (Imam al-Ṣādiq on Q 17:71): those who recognized the Imam of their age will be called with their Imam; those who followed a false claimant will be called with the leader they followed. The verse applies universally — to every age, not only to the Prophet's immediate community. The implication: recognition of the legitimate Imam of each age is eschatologically constitutive, not merely theologically preferable.

Premise 3: Biḥār al-Anwār (Imam al-Bāqir): man māta wa-lam yaʿrif imāmahu māta mītat jāhiliyya — "whoever dies without recognizing their Imam dies the death of jāhiliyya." This hadith — with parallel transmissions in Sunni sources (Musnad Aḥmad, Ibn Ḥibbān) — establishes recognition of the living Imam as the condition for dying in a state other than jāhiliyya (pre-Islamic ignorance). The implication: formal Islamic identity without recognition of the living hādī does not eliminate the jāhiliyya-state.

Conclusion: Q 17:71 reveals that the hādī/Imam function is not merely historically important but eschatologically constitutive: the criterion of final calling on Judgment Day is organized around the Imam-relationship. Biḥār al-Anwār's hadith (with cross-sectarian parallel transmissions) elevates recognition of the Imam of each age from a theological opinion to a structural eschatological necessity — making the hādī the axis of both this-worldly guidance and eschatological identification.
Sources: Q 17:71; Al-Kāfī, Kitāb al-Ḥujja (Imam al-Ṣādiq on Q 17:71); Biḥār al-Anwār, Vol. 8 (Imam al-Bāqir — mītat jāhiliyya hadith); Musnad Aḥmad (parallel Sunni transmission).
HADI-005 Grade B — Ibn ʿArabī, al-Futūḥāt · Al-Kāfī · Q 7:180 Akbarian Layer VI

The Imam as the Living Locus of the Divine Name al-Hādī

Premise 1: Q 7:180: "wa-lillāhi al-asmāʾu al-ḥusnā fa-dʿūhu bihā" — "To Allah belong the most beautiful names — so call upon Him by them." Q 17:110 adds al-Raḥmān as an equivalent name. The divine names (asmāʾ al-ḥusnā) are not merely descriptive epithets — in the Akbarian framework, each name is a divine reality that requires a locus of manifestation (maẓhar) in the created world.

Premise 2: Ibn ʿArabī (al-Futūḥāt al-Makkiyya, on the Qutb): the divine name al-Hādī (the Guide) requires a living maẓhar — a human locus through whom guidance manifests in the world. This living locus is the Qutb al-aqṭāb (pole of poles) or the perfect human being (al-insān al-kāmil) of each age. The Imami tradition identifies this as the Imam — the living manifestation of the divine name al-Hādī in the world of material creation.

Premise 3: Al-Kāfī (Imam al-Ṣādiq): "naḥnu wajhu Allāh alladhī yutawajjahu bihi" — "We are the face of Allah by which one turns toward Him." The Imam is not metaphorically Allah's face — in the Akbarian-Imami synthesis, the Imam is the maẓhar (locus of manifestation) of the divine name al-Hādī, without whose presence the name has no living locus in the world of creation.

Conclusion: The convergence of Ibn ʿArabī's doctrine of divine name-loci (maẓāhir al-asmāʾ) with the Imami doctrine of the Imam as ḥujja Allāh produces the following: the divine name al-Hādī requires a living human locus of manifestation in each age; the Imam is that locus; the earth is never without this locus (Al-Kāfī: al-arḍu lā takhlu min ḥujja). This is not merely political leadership or scholarly guidance — it is the ontological maintenance of the divine name's manifestation in the world.
Sources: Q 7:180; Ibn ʿArabī, al-Futūḥāt al-Makkiyya, Chapter 73 (on divine names and their loci); Al-Kāfī, Kitāb al-Ḥujja (Imam al-Ṣādiq — naḥnu wajhu Allāh).
HADI-006 Grade B — Al-Kāfī (Imam al-Ṣādiq on ghayba) · Q 21:105 Imami Layer VII

The Imam's Guidance Persists Through Occultation — The Hidden Hujja

Premise 1: Al-Kāfī (Imam al-Ṣādiq on the ghayba): the occultation of the Imam does not mean the cessation of his function — the Imam in occultation benefits the community as the sun benefits the world from behind clouds (ka-l-shamsi idhā ghāba ʿan al-abṣār). The ḥujja function is active whether the Imam is physically present or hidden: his existence maintains the earth's ontological axis.

Premise 2: Al-Kāfī (Imam al-Ṣādiq): "al-arḍu lā takhlu min ḥujja — wa-law lā dhālika la-sākhat al-arḍu bi-ahlihā" — "the earth is never without a ḥujja — and if it were, the earth would swallow its inhabitants." The Imam's function in ghayba is not merely theological — it is cosmological: maintaining the created world's stability through the presence of the divine ḥujja, even in hidden form.

Premise 3: Q 21:105: "wa-laqad katabnā fī al-zabūri min baʿdi al-dhikri anna al-arḍa yarithuhā ʿibādiya al-ṣāliḥūn" — "We wrote in the Psalms after the reminder: the earth shall be inherited by My righteous servants." The ghayba is not a permanent state — it is a period of preparation for the earth's inheritance by the righteous. The hidden Imam guides the community toward readiness for this inheritance even through the ghayba, through the four deputies (nuwwāb) during the minor occultation and through walāya-connection during the major occultation.

Conclusion: The Imam's guidance function does not cease with the ghayba (occultation) — it continues in hidden form. Al-Kāfī's sun-behind-clouds analogy and the cosmological ḥujja-function (the earth cannot exist without the ḥujja) establish that occultation is a mode of the Imam's presence, not his absence. Q 21:105's inheritance of the earth by the righteous is the endpoint toward which the hidden guidance of the ghayba period moves — making the community's maintenance of its walāya-connection during occultation essential preparation for the return.
Sources: Al-Kāfī, Kitāb al-Ḥujja (Imam al-Ṣādiq on ghayba — sun-behind-clouds analogy + al-arḍu lā takhlu); Q 21:105; Biḥār al-Anwār, Vol. 52 (ghayba and its functions).
